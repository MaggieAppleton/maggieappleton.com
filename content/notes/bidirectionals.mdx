<IntroParagraph>

With the recent rise of <a href="https://roamresearch.com/">Roam Research</a>, the idea of bi-directional linking is having a bit of a moment. 

</IntroParagraph>

We're all very used to the _mono-directional_ link the World Wide Web is built around. They act as one-way pointers we follow in a linear sequence. 

While we can link to any site, the destination page hasn't a clue we've done so.

We set up all these single direction paths, trying to signal relevance and context, only to have the other side completely ignore our efforts.

Our monolinks are trying to establish relationships in vain. 


<FullWidthImage width="900px" src="https://res.cloudinary.com/dg3gyk0gu/image/upload/v1589455927/maggieappleton.com/notes/Hyperlinks.png" />


We're starting to look around our mono-linked environment, and wonder why it's so hard to surface relevant, contextual relationships.

Manually interlinking content takes an awful lot of human curation and effort. Effort we should probably slog off onto our systems.

A _bi-directional_ link has **social awareness** - it knows about other pages or 'nodes' that point to it, and can make them visible to people. This means we get a two-way conversation flowing between our web locations.

### Our fabled **bi-directional link** is not new.

This concept goes all the way back to 1945, when **Vannevar Bush** dreamed up the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memex">Memex</a> machine.

 Vannevar outlined this hypothetical gadget in the essay <a href="https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/1945/07/as-we-may-think/303881/">As We May Think</a> â€“ a system capable of "associative indexing... whereby any item may be caused at will to select immediately and automatically another... [so that] numerous items have been thus joined together to form a trail."

<br />


<FullWidthImage width="800px" src="https://res.cloudinary.com/dg3gyk0gu/image/upload/c_scale,w_960/v1589545798/maggieappleton.com/notes/memex.jpg" />

<SmallCenter>

 <h5 style="text-align: center; margin-top: 1em; ">Vannevar even created a small informative diagram of this desk-bound vision. Marketing chops 101.</h5>

 </SmallCenter>

Vannevar had a fairly impressive imagination, especially given that digital computers had only come into existence 5 years earlier. Most were still the domain of large military operations like Bletchley Park, and seen as inconveniently large calculators.

Implementing a wildly interactive computational personal knowledge base wasn't much of an option.

So the idea went into hiberation, and didn't resurface until personal computing began blooming in the early sixties. <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ted_Nelson">Ted Nelson</a> took inspiration from Vannevar's ideas in 1965 when he coined the term *hypertext* to describe his vision for a sprawling network of interlinking text.

Nelson planned to implement these hypertextual dreams in his perpetually-imminent Project Xanadu. <Footnote idName={1}>If you have some time, this is quite the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Xanadu">internet history rabbit hole</a> to run down. <br /> <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=F0NgV3yNd2o">Ted Nelson is on another level</a>.</Footnote>

The Xanadu system imagined that every sentence, block, and page would be part of a vast bi-directionally linked network:

<br />

<FullWidthImage width="900px" src="https://res.cloudinary.com/dg3gyk0gu/image/upload/c_scale,w_1000/v1589545780/maggieappleton.com/notes/xanadu.png" />

</Center>

Suffice to say, Xanadu didn't pan out.

Instead we got the less fancy, but far more real and useable World Wide Web that currently does not support bi-directionals on an infrastructre level.

While Sir Tim Berner's Lee <a href="https://www.w3.org/DesignIssues/Topology.html">wrote himself a note</a> debating their pros and cons back in 1999, there is an obvious design issue with letting two-way connections flow freely around the web.  

If _every_ site that linked to yours was visible on your page, and you had no control over who could and couldn't link to you, it is not hard to imagine the Trollish implications...

Figuring out how we might filter, moderate, and set permissions around link visibility turned into <a href="https://dl.acm.org/doi/epdf/10.1145/74224.74236">quite the challenge</a>. The <a href="https://dl.acm.org/doi/epdf/10.1145/74224.74229">design details</a> <a href="https://dl.acm.org/doi/epdf/10.1145/74224.74245">grew complex</a>.

It became clear implementing the Web with simpler mono-directional links was the right thing to do, given that its creators wanted universal adoption.  Lots of people are still mad about it. Let's not venture too far down that historical wormhole.

> **The TLDR is technology is hard**.

Until <a href="https://www.wired.com/1995/06/xanadu/">Xanadu ships</a> and we're all immersed in the universe of multi-linked, version-controlled nodes of remixable micro-content (that somehow solves the problems of permissions and moderation), there are still plenty of ways we can resurrect the possibility of bi-directional links on the web...

## That's all very cool, but how am I supposed to build this?


Thankfully, setting up your own public gardening bi-directional Memex doesn't involve Xanadu.

One fantastic option for non-developers is based around a personal wiki system called <a href="https://tiddlywiki.com/">TiddlyWiki</a>.  Anne-Laure Le Cunff wrote up an easy-to-follow guide to getting <a href="https://nesslabs.com/tiddlywiki-beginner-tutorial"> your own up and running</a>.

For those of us here for the hyper-customised, over-engineered Javascript solution (that would be me ðŸ™Œ), the <a href="https://www.gatsbyjs.org/">Gatsby.js</a> community has a number of active gardening enthusiasts building themes and plugins.

I built mine using Aengus McMillin's <a href="https://github.com/aengusmcmillin/gatsby-theme-brain">gatsby-theme-brain</a>. Aengus has documented the theme well and it's not too challenging to implement.

I also curate a list of tools for [[Digital Gardening]] on this [Github repo](https://github.com/MaggieAppleton/digital-gardeners/)

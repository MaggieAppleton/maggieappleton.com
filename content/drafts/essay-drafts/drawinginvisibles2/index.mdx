Now that we have a solid understanding of how visual metaphors work, it's time to start building one.

Our current case study is based around <a href="https://twitter.com/mikesherov">Mike Sherov</a>'s course on <a href="https://egghead.io/courses/web-security-essentials?af=54fd64">Web Security Essentials</a>.
To quickly refresh on our goal, we're creating an illustration that **clearly communicates the idea of "Web Security"**

We've established that *Web Security* is not a single physical _thing_ we can point to and sketch out on a bit of paper.
It's instead an amorphorous, evolving blob of cultural concepts. And we're going to need to find a good **metaphor** to represent that cultural blob.

Our first order of business here is **cultural research**.

## Thinking like a Cultural Anthropologist

We often think illustration is all about creating visual shape and form. Everyone wants to dive into putting pencil to paper, stylus to tablet, and cursor to vector point.

But I want to talk about a very particular approach that's founded in __cultural anthropology__.
It's probably not a common approach, but I'm coming at this from a weird angle.

<p>I have no legit qualifications in design or illustration. My bachelor's degree and <a href="https://www.ucl.ac.uk/anthropology/study/graduate-taught/digital-anthropology-msc">current master's degree</a> are both in Anthropology.<Footnote idName={1}>A quick definition of anthropology. It's not the sort related to digging up neanderthals (archeology)</Footnote> I started learning illustration and traditional drawing in my early twenties because I feel a deep urge to explain cultural concepts and social science theory visually.<Footnote idName={2}>From the ether? Subconscious yearnings are odd forces.</Footnote></p>

<p>I'm still an anthropologist. I just wrap it in the shiny facade of illustration because no one hires anthropologists.<Footnote idName={3}>Except the US military if you're into studying effective methods of social shaming and developing psychological torture techniques</Footnote></p> 

While the medium of illustration surely matters here, the *cultural message* beneath an image is really the interesting part.
This anthropological form of illustration is a way of doing [Semiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiotics) - a fancy anthropological word for the study of signs and symbols.

Humans will turn everything and anything into a symbol.  
Nothing is what it seems.  
Cereal choices are socio-economic markers.   
Shoes are political affiliations.  
Emojis have four layers of sub-text. <span aria-role="img">üêù</span> <span aria-role="img">üçÜ</span> <span aria-role="img">üêê</span> <span aria-role="img">‚ùÑÔ∏è</span>

Whatever concept we're trying to meaningfully communicate, it comes pre-baked with a set of symbolic associations that we need to pay attention to.

Your audience does not live in a void. They, like you, are active members of a cultural world that is constantly creating and passing around a stream of symbolic concepts in the form of buzzwords, technical jargon, tweets, overly opinionated reddit comments, icons, illustrations, memes, GIFs, and bad stock photos. It's [The Zeitgeist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeitgeist).

Our task is to __map out the cultural field__ to get a genuine understanding of what our intended audience associates with the concept.

Finding the right visual metaphor is a glorious scavenger hunt through language, iconography, and cultural connections.

## Part Three - Cultural Ethnography

If we want to find out the general cultural conceptions around "Web Security," we head to the native watering holes of the internet - Hacker News,reddit, and twitter.
What I'm looking for are overarching cultural tropes, touchstones, and metaphorical language.

[r/cybersecurity](https://www.reddit.com/r/cybersecurity/)
[r/websecurity](https://www.reddit.com/r/websecurity/)

Some things that jump out ‚Äì "penetration testing" "risk management" "secure field login" "third-party vendors" "XSS" "vulnerability" "injection"

Overall noticing empahsis on entry points like login forms. It's noticing we speak about websites as contained objects that have physical boundaries and vulnerable entryways into that container.
Chinks in the armour. Gatewatys into the fortness. Cracks in the walls. Underground tunnels into mission control. Injections into the human body - our skin is our container.
